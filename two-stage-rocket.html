2 Stage Rocket

*Note: This project is a work in progress, and will be updated as I go along

Overview

           Instead of rebuilding the same rocket for my L2 certification over again, I decided it would be more fun and technically interesting to start working on a staged rocket instead, and launched the first stage test as my L2 certification
             

Part 1: ​Design

       This in part came because I wanted to keep my L2 certification under a certain altitude and, instead of making it heavier, found that I could also add a second set of fins. I decided that instead of rebuilding a simple rocket just to get a certification, I would integrate my L2 certification launch into a test launch for a 2 stage rocket. For this: I designed a rocket with an upper stage connected to another tube with a coupler.
         The one design change I made was to adjust the top fin assembly to allow airflow when there was no motor in it. I reduced the area of each centering ring and made holes in the bulkhead to allow for airflow to get through. When I launch as a 2 stage, all I have to do is add a thrust plate to the upper stage and it will work as my other rockets

Initial design of the rocket

Part 2: Building

          For this rocket, I used simple manufacturing methods to build it. I used my typical process for cutting out the fins of using a laser cutter and then epoxying together the assembly. For the nosecone, I used the same design I have used for my previous rockets with a 2 part 3d printed nosecone that screws together. I then cut fin slits in the side with a handsaw and box cutters, and drilled holes for the coupler with a drill and connected the parts screws. Overall, for the first airframe test, I opted for a simple rocket.

Part 3: Airframe Test

          Unfortunately, the airframe test did not go as planned. During launch, the fins on the bottom ended up being too large and snapped due to fin flutter. The rest of the rocket went up as normal, and the parachute deployed as normal. 
broken fin tips

Part 4: Composite Rebuild

              To remedy the fin situation, I decided to remake the fins out of composites. I decided that I would use my school's composite lab to make them from scratch. 
              The first idea was to make them our of pure carbon fiber. After making a test article, I discovered two flaws with this decision. First, it was hard to get the fin into the correct shape. The fibers would keep slipping, making the resultant fin very out of shape. Second, the amount of layers needed to make a fin as large as I wanted was too, and would take far too long of a time to manufacture. To remedy this, I decided to use wood as a core for mine.
                 To do this, I cut out layers of carbon weave. I then layered small amounts of epoxy on both sides of the wood core, then layered on the carbon fiber before putting more epoxy on the outside under a fume hood. Then, with 3 layers of supporting material around it, I vacuum bagged the parts and waiting overnight. To get rid of excess material, I used a Dremel over a downdraft table to make sure I didn't inhale toxic dust

Part 5: Relaunch

             After assembling my new bottom section, I was able to launch my rocket again. This time I had greater success. The increased shear modulus of the fins reduced the risk of fin flutter, and they ended up not fluttering off. The rest of the launch was also successful, and landed only with minor damage to the coupler due to the parachute release getting caught to the chute. To reduce the risk of this in the future, I will redesign the deployment and create a dual deployment system. 
             Overall, I was able to get a L2 certification with this success, and was happy with the result.

Part 6: Next Steps

             Now that the airframe has been proven to work together, I will now work towards the staging electronics. For the next flights, I plan on designing a telemetry system that will stage the rocket and light the new motor given that the rocket is within a acceptable range of velocity and angular orientation after the first stage. I will also add a dual deployment system on this electronics suite. Significant ground testing will be conducted before this is attempted
