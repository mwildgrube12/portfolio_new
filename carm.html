CARM: Tufts Rocketry's 1st IREC Entry

​

Overview

           In the 2023-2024 year, I decided that the next step of the Tufts Rocketry team was to pursue the Spaceport America Cup. After my suggestion and conversations, me and my co-lead decided that it was the next best step for the club. We reorganized the upper levels of the club to prepare for this, and as we have no electrical engineers at the time, I took an extra role as Electrical lead in addition to overall co-lead, and we added new positions such as Mechanical lead, Telemetry lead, Propulsion lead, and Recovery lead, and reorganized the club to accommodate a more team oriented project. As Overall Co-lead and Electrical, I helped organize the team structure, double the amount of members we had, and fully designed and built a flight computer, among other contributions.

​Overall Design

           Our project, which came to be know as CARM, was a six-inch-diameter, single-stage fiberglass rocket designed with a dual-separation, dual-deploy system. Our mission was to carry a prototype 2U CubeSAT to 10,000 feet and test custom onboard flight computers, which I designed. We chose the six-inch diameter as it allowed us to maintain the CubeSAT form factor while also accommodating a backup inert “dummy” payload. With a focus on performance and flexibility, we opted for a 98 mm motor for the IREC launch, while keeping the ability to use smaller 76 mm motors for lower-altitude test flights through a motor adapter. Fiberglass was selected for the airframe due to its strength, durability, RF transparency, and widespread use in high-power rocketry.
​       Throughout the CARM project, we prioritized SRAD components to enhance performance and maximize student involvement. However, the airframe materials were sourced from commercial vendors, as our team does not yet have the capability to manufacture advanced composites in-house. The airframe design leaned on standard COTS fiberglass components to define mass and dimensions.
         We embraced a design philosophy centered on simplicity, reliability, and robustness, favoring a bit of extra weight for safety. As this was our first entry into the IREC, this approach gave us a higher chance of success, while also preparing the vehicle for off-nominal flight conditions like high temperatures, deploy speeds, and landing impacts. While some components, like the airframe bulkheads and fin can, ended up heavier than initially planned, this trade-off provided a valuable buffer for added security in challenging scenarios. In future iterations, we plan to reduce these as we get better at manufacturing and more use to the design.

Electronics Design

           During the 2024-2025 school year , I gradually developed an Electronics and Avionics team for our Spaceport America team. These custom flight computers, which we called "Scallion Pancake," were designed to take over the primary recovery tasks. Over the course of the year, we went through 3 main versions of the flight computer as we got more and more comfortable with PCB design and gradually improved upon the design to create our final computers. The first versions were rife with issues, including poor space usage, poor protections from shorting, and used parts that were all through hole and unnecessarily large. On the second and third versions, all of this was improved and smaller, cheaper parts were used, space was much more optimized, and in general more attention was paid to the robustness of the system.         
        The final board was as 4-layer PCB designed to integrate the features of the Adafruit LORA feather with spots to attach the GPS, barometric sensor, temperature sensor, and an IMU. It also was design to accommodate an off board thermocouple and temperature sensors. In addition, it was designed to send back this data to a ground station as well as store it in an on-board SD card. It was then designed to use the data to control a state determination machine that would tell the flight computer when to deploy parachutes. It also came with the features of a buzzer to indicate continuity on the charges from afar and polarity protection for the battery connections. It also was designed to be able to send basic commands to a camera to turn them on and off during launch.
          The boards rely on open-source Arduino libraries to interface with the sensors and handle data collection. For precision, the flight data undergoes a two-step Kalman filtering process, merging information from barometric altitude, accelerometer, and gyroscope readings to determine key metrics like altitude, vertical velocity, and acceleration. These metrics, along with temperature changes inside the avionics bay, help identify different phases of the rocket’s flight. Based on these phases, the system decides when to deploy the drogue and main parachutes.

Testing

             For the rocket and for the electronics, we completed a thorough testing campaign to ensure that all of our systems were working before the competition. For this, we did a total of 3 test launches, as well as ground testing. The ground tests that were completed included deployment tests, GPS range testing, and state machine testing. All of these allowed for greater confidence in 
               On the first test launch, we successfully were able to launch our airframe. This was the first airframe of this size we had build, and we ended up making it a bit heavier and beefier than expected. Nonetheless, it had a nominal launch. However, as we were setting up the flight computers, we managed to short the flight computers, and were unable to test them. Despite this setback, this failure allowed me to redesign the entire board so that we could have an improved system for further launches
           By the second test launch, we launched the rocket with the improved flight computers and more powerful motors. This launch was less ideal; nonetheless, the rocket was launched undamaged. The main parachute got caught, which caused it to fall harder than planned, but it didn't damage the rocket. In addition, we were able to launch the flight computers and get data on one of them. The data, however, was processed incorrectly during the flight and thus the state machine didn't properly function.
            We did a third launch, which was with some new parts of the rocket and an edited flight computer, but this got stuck in a tree for 2 weeks, which caused the data on our flight computers to become corrupted.

Competition

                In June of 2024, we drove out our rocket to New Mexico for the 2024 Spaceport America Cup. For this competition, we completed a 49 page technical report, for which I was responsible for the electronics and testing sections, and presented our design to a group of judges. We showcased our rocket at a convention in the first day to a group of international participants, and passed the safety checks. We then prepared our rocket to launch. Due to external factors like a dust storm and cloud cover, we got delayed in our launch; however, we were able to get the rocket on the pad at around 10 am on the 3rd day, and launch around 3 hours later. The launch was largely a success, and we were able to recover the rocket at 6pm the day off and present it to the judges for a perfect recovery score. Below are pictures of the launch day.

Results

              Overall, we placed 33rd place overall out of 154 entries. While we won no awards, we are extremely proud that on our first ever competition rocket we were able to successfully build and design a rocket that was capable of going and competing at the cup. This was a great step foward for the club, and a very good showing for a first year team.
              On a more qualitative not, we had some failures that weren't reflected in this score. On launch day, errors with the flight computers means we got no flight data from our custom boards, which was rather disappointing. In addition, we has some minor issues with the parachutes. These things will be heavily used to improve our design next year, as well as learnings such as longer-than-expected on pad times and new ideas of how to get more camera footage

Next Steps

              I am incredibly proud of the work my team and I accomplished at the Spaceport America Cup, even though the electronics didn’t go exactly as planned. Over the past year, we incorporated breakout boards to meet project deadlines, but this experience has taught me a lot about the importance of custom solutions. The challenges we faced have inspired me to take our efforts further, and I’m excited to lead the development of a fully integrated PCB for next year’s project, minimizing the reliance on breakout boards. I’m confident that these improvements will significantly enhance our performance.
