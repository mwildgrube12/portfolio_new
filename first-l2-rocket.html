First L2 Attempt

Overview

       After my L1 launch, I wanted to try to move on to a L2 rocket so that the club could move forward with bigger rockets so that eventually we can have an L3 certified rocketeer and go to the Spaceport America in New Mexico. In addition, I wanted to launch a rocket that went higher and utilize dual deployment so that I could make more effective rockets in the future​

Part 1: ​Design

       This rocket actually used a motor with a lower average thrust than my old rocket, with a average thrust of 150 N as apposed to my L1 certification flight had 200N. However, unlike my L1 flight, this was predicted to go over twice as high to a height of 6600 feet, so I used extra caution. The rocket used a motor called a 949-J150-P, meaning in addition to the average thrust of 150 N it had 949 N/s of impulse and had no delay charge for parachute deployment. This, as well as the height it was going, meant I had to design a deployment system separate from my motor. For this I used a dual deployment altimeter, which set off a drogue parachute at apogee and a main parachute at 500 feet to prevent too much drift away from the launch site in the wind.​​
Planned Simulation of Launch Data

Part 2: Building

        This rocket needed a tiny big more thought put into the manufacturing due to the higher total impulse. I overdesigned the rocket a bit in hopes of relaunch with higher average thrust. The significant changes include the following:
  • A thrust plate was added on the bottom of the last centering ring to redistribute load on the body tube better​
  • 3 eyehooks were used instead of 1 to evenly distribute the load on the fin assembly.
  • 2 sections for parachutes so that they could be deployed at different times
  • a section for electronics to separate them from the area where the charges would blow
​Most the rest the design was the same, and I reused most of the same manufacturing techniques as my last rocket, using the laser cutter for the fins, the 3d printer for the nose cone, the table saw and miter saw to cut the body tube, and eboxy to put most of it together.

Part 3: Launch

        The launch, unfortunately was unsuccessful. We launch on a cold day in upper Vermont, and my rocket went of at about 1:30pm. The way up was pretty smooth; however we lost sight of it about at the cloud level (I am not sure if we misjudged the cloud height and pierced it or not) and never regained sight of it. One person at the site said they may have seen it fall without deploying either parachute in the direction of its last know trajectory, but I looked for 2 hours and never found it (likely because it went much farther than expected).

Part 3: Lessons Learned (the hard way)

             I learned some very important lessons this launch. Losing a rocket completely taught me a lot about precautions I should have taken, and am planning to apply a lot of them to my next launch:
  •  Use a GPS tracker for rockets that could go outside of my sight range (>5000 feet, about) so I can track it
  • Bring Binoculars so that we can see rockets farther away
  • Paint my rocket in high contrast, easy to see colors instead of whatever looks cool so that it is easier to see
  • put buzzers on my rocket so I can use sound and sight to look for it
In general, I learned a lot and will be much more careful about my rocket launches in the future
