Team Rocket

Overview

           My co-leader and I created on a project to construct a larger and more ambitious team rocket. This served two key purposes: honing our ability to efficiently delegate tasks within a rocket's construction process and advancing our proficiency in crafting intricate components collaboratively. To realize these objectives, we meticulously engineered a rocket designed to approach the height threshold of the nearest high-altitude launch site. We meticulously developed a K motor-propelled rocket featuring a 3-inch body tube, enabling it to attain an impressive altitude of 9,500 feet, just bellow the 10,000 foot ceiling of the site. This project epitomized our dedication to tackling complex engineering tasks while nurturing collaborative skills.​​

Part 1: ​Design

       For this project, the main goal was to "make a rocket that goes high," but we also wanted to try our hands at making a sturdier, more reliable design. For this, we decided to do two main things differently: we wanted to make stronger fins and make a better, more reliable, electronics.
        For the fins, we designed them out of Aluminum instead of plywood. This was because we were launching a lot higher than before and we wanted them to be much more secure, especially since we have had problems on landing before. We designed smaller fins and a fin assembly out of aluminum, and made a separate bulkhead instead of the combined fin assembly we had with the wood fins. This was done because the motor tube was a lot longer than it previously was and it was not weight efficient when using fins of denser material.  
       For the electronics, building off the unknown failure of my last rocket (that, though testing of the team rocket, I figured out was probably ejection charge failure) I wanted to create a more reliable system for recovery, including finding it. We also wanted to start to add more data collection to the rocket. I designed the electronics bay to improve all of this. Inside the electronics bay I had 3 main electronic components: a dual deployment altimeter, a GPS tracker, and a thermocouple data logger. The GPS and the altimeter were hooked up to 2 9v batteries in parallel, while the logger had an internal battery powering it independently. I also designed the electronics bay so that changing deployment charges was simpler so that more systems could be tested easily. The thermocouple was attached to the motor so that we could monitor motor temperature. The purpose of the GPS tracker was for easier recovery so that we didn't lose it like I lost my L2 rocket.

Part 2: Building

          For this rocket, I used a lot of similar manufacturing methods to previous rockets and projects but improved upon them. However, since we changed a lot of materials used in this rocket, we had to use a few new methods.
            First, to build the fin assembly, we used a water jet to cut out the centering rings and the fins out of 6016 aluminum. Then, we tapped holes in the centering rings and put it all together with threaded beams and nuts. We then used epoxy clay to hold this together, before using more to hold it into the bottom section of the body tube. This fin assembly did not have a bulkhead for the parachute like the rest of the rockets the club has build, so we made one out of laser cut wood and glued it to the top of where the motor was supposed to reach with two eyehooks for the parachute line. We also left a hole in it so that we could go back later and insert the thermocouple
          For the electronics bay, I designed the 3 main instruments to fit on a sliding board in a 8 inch section in the middle of the rocket. On one side I attached the GPS and the altimeter board, which I wired up to 2 batteries in parallel to have redundancy in power supply. I then attached them to the back of the board. To turn this part on and off, I installed a button switch that could be pressed from the outside. The thermocouple ran on an internal battery independently of the other two electronics, and was attached with hooks onto the board.

Part 3: Testing

          Since my previous rocket was lost, we did a lot more prelaunch testing on this rocket to ensure it would not get lost or fail. The main two ground tests that we did were testing the GPS and testing the ejection charges. 
         To test the GPS, we turned on the GPS and had one person walk to an undetermined place, and had another person find them only using the GPS. From this, we determined that the GPS was functional, and we learned its approximate accuracy.
       For the ejection charges, we had a slightly more complicated testing. Since we were unsure if my dual deploy rocket's ejection charge even went off, we wanted to ensure we had a large enough charge to blow out the parachute with a significant margin of error. To do this, we measured out 10 black powder charges, 2 of each weight. The weights were approximately 0.3 g, 0.35 g, 0.4 g, 0.5g and 0.55g. We then simulated going above deployment altitude by using a vacuum to pull out all of the air from the electronics bay and then letting all the air back in.
          After a few failures, we had to combine some premeasured charges to get a charge of ~0.7 g to eject the parachute. Since we had so much struggle to get the ejection to go, we decided that we would use ~1 g of powder on the actual launch to ensure that we had deployment.

Part 4: Launch

                We prepared most of rocket the night beforehand so that we could launch the rocket bright and early when we got to the location. We launched at about 10:30 am. Unfortunately, the launch was unsuccessful. After liftoff at about ~500 feet, the bottom section of the body tube had an explosion that caused the bottom to shear apart. Shortly after, the rest of the rocket fell apart

Part 5: Failure Analysis

              After we got back from the launch, I did a bit of failure analysis to see what went wrong.  
              Directly after the launch, one of the other people attending the rocket launch attested that some similar motos had had O-ring failures before caused by leaking of hot gas in the sides of the grains that degrade the O-rings. For the case of our failure, this failure has a high probability of having occurred. After disassembling the motor assembly, I discovered that the top had likely had a failed O-ring that caused the delayed deploy charge to improperly fire early, which blew out the side of the motor and the body tube.
              The O-ring failure only usually occur on the top; however, our motor also failed on the bottom. The failure of the bottom could be a compounded effect from the top O-ring failure, or another failure of the O-ring on the bottom, as part of the O-ring was destroyed on the bottom as well. It could also be from a grain failure on the bottom that blew out part of the metal on the bottom.

Part 6: Next Steps

              Although the launch did not go the way we wanted, nor did it give a good test of our systems, we learned a lot about the process of making a team rocket from this experience, and on proper testing procedures. I plan to use these experiences on a personal, 2 stage rocket and a team rocket in a competition, hopefully at IREC next year
